<br> #### Week 10: Machine Building and End Effectors For this week, I wanted to design a simple drawing machine. Not a full drawing machine, but a machine that could move a pen around a piece of paper and make marks on it. I was inspired by this drawing machine on the website: <a>https://roberthart56.github.io/SCFAB/SC_lab/S-12machine/index.html</a> To accomplish this, I decided I would create a design where two stepper motors are at the corners of a cardboard base. They would be tied with string to a piece of wood with a pen stuck in it. That wood would also be connected to a weight that falls off the edge of the table, like in the example. Then I would stabilize the pen in the wood by tying it with three strings to the wood stabilizer. PEN COMPLEX To make the wood stabilizer, I used the CNC mill to make a small 5 by 5 cm square with 8 5mm diameter holes in each corner and on each side. In the center there is a 9.7mm diameter hole, this is the width of the pen I was using just before it is at the grip-part of the pen. I put 3 strings around the pen and tied them to the stabilizer base, on three of the sides. I then tied string around the base and a small metal weight. ![image of pen and string](./PenandString.png) ![image of pen and stabilizer 1](./PokingthroughPenandString.png) ![image of weight](./Weight.png) MOTOR SET-UP ![image of motor setup](./MotorSetUp.png) I set up each motor to have a power and ground from the Arduino Uno board, one on Vin pin and one on the 5V pin. I connected each to its own L9110 board. And to pins 11, 10, 9, 8 and 6, 5, 4, 3 I used this page on the website as a guide to wire my motors: <a>https://nathanmelenbrink.github.io/ps70/10_machines/index.html</a> I then, once I had figured out the code, I tied each of them to a string that was also tied to the stabilizer, and wrapped that string around the motors. CALIBRATION I was not able to code a way for the stepper motors to reliably move themselves to the right origin every time I power it on, as I am still figuring out how to reliably get the machine to write specific distances, and working out the impact of the two motors spinning at different speeds. Instead, to calibrate the machine, you hold the square down on a line on the cardboard base and wrap the string tightly around the two motors. Then you add your piece of paper, and you are good to go!
